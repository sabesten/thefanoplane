Chapter 1

From Luoshu to Fano: The $G_2$-Equivariant Projection $S^7 \to \mathbb{Z}_7^3$

{section}{Chapter 1. From Luoshu to Fano}

The problem of constructing a symmetry-preserving discretisation of a high-dimensional sphere is a central technical difficulty in discrete geometry and topological quantum field theory. One wishes to map the points of a sphere continuously onto a finite lattice in such a way that the full symmetry group of the sphere acts equivariantly on the lattice. The exceptional Lie group $G_2$ furnishes a natural setting for this problem: it acts isometrically on the $7$--sphere $S^7$, and $S^7$ is diffeomorphic to the coset $\Spin(7)/G_2$. This chapter lays the algebraic foundations needed for the equivariant projection construction developed in later sections. We begin with the ancient $3\times3$ Luoshu magic square, show that its magic property is a linear condition over the field $\mathbb{F}_3$, and raise the question of whether an analogous linear construction exists for order $7$ in three dimensions. The answer leads us through the finite projective plane of order $2$, the octonion algebra, and finally to the explicit construction of a $G_2$--invariant orthogonal projection $S^7\to\mathbb{Z}_7^3$.

The Luoshu as an $\mathbb{F}_3$ Magic Square

The Lo Shu (Luoshu) is the $3\times3$ array

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 9 & 2\\ 3 & 5 & 7\\ 8 & 1 & 6 \end{pmatrix} $$

filled with the integers $1,\dots ,9$, each appearing exactly once. The magic property is that the sum of the three entries in each row, each column, and each of the two main diagonals equals $15$. It is convenient to relabel the entries by the elements of the prime field $\mathbb{F}_3=\{0,1,2\}$, identifying $0$ with $3$, $1$ with $4$, and $2$ with $5$, respectively. With this convention the array becomes

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0\\ 2 & 1 & 2\\ 0 & 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquador equivalently\qquad \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0\\ 2 & 1 & 2\\ 0 & 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, $$

where all arithmetic is now performed in $\mathbb{F}_3$.

Let $\mathbb{F}_3^2$ denote the $2$--dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}_3$, whose $9$ elements we identify with the positions $(i,j)\in\{0,1,2\}^2$ of the $3\times3$ grid. The affine group $\AG(2,3)=\mathbb{F}_3^2\rtimes\GL(2,3)$ acts transitively on these $9$ positions; the orbits of this action are precisely the $12$ affine lines of $\AG(2,3)$ (each line contains $3$ points, and there are $4$ lines in each of the $3$ parallelism classes).

Definition

A function $f:\AG(2,3)\to\mathbb{F}_3$ is affine-linear if there exist $a,b,c\in\mathbb{F}_3$ such that $f(x,y)=ax+by+c$ for all $(x,y)\in\mathbb{F}_3^2$.

Proposition

The Luoshu labelling $(§eq:luoshu-f3)$ is an affine-linear function $f:\AG(2,3)\to\mathbb{F}_3$ with coefficients $(a,b)=(2,1)$ and $c=0$, i.e. $f(x,y)=2x+y\pmod 3$. Consequently, $f$ is constant on every affine line of $\AG(2,3)$; equivalently, $f$ takes each value $0,1,2$ exactly once on each affine line.

\begin{proof} Direct substitution shows $f(0,0)=0$, $f(0,1)=1$, $f(0,2)=2$, $f(1,0)=2$, $f(1,1)=0$, $f(1,2)=1$, $f(2,0)=1$, $f(2,1)=2$, $f(2,2)=0$, which agrees with the array $(§eq:luoshu-f3)$. Since an affine line in $\AG(2,3)$ is a set of the form $\{(x_0,y_0)+t(u,v):t\in\mathbb{F}_3\}$ for some direction vector $(u,v)\neq(0,0)$, the value $f$ takes on that line is $f(x_0,y_0)+t(au+bv)$; because $au+bv\neq0$ (as $(u,v)\neq(0,0)$ and $(a,b)=(2,1)\neq(0,0)$), the expression $au+bv$ runs through all of $\mathbb{F}_3$ as $t$ varies, and hence $f$ is constant on the line only when we consider the value modulo $3$ in the sense that each fibre $f^{-1}(c)$ meets each affine line in exactly one point. In the integer formulation this is exactly the magic-square condition. ∎ \end{prop}

The magic property of the Luoshu is therefore a linear algebraic fact over $\mathbb{F}_3$, not a numerical coincidence. This observation suggests a natural generalisation: for which primes $p$ does there exist a linear function $\Phi:\AG(3,p)\to\mathbb{F}_p$ that is a bijection on every affine line? The present monograph is centred on the case $p=7$ and on the associated construction of a $G_2$--equivariant projection from the $7$--sphere to the lattice $\mathbb{Z}_7^3$.

The Fano Plane and Projective Geometry over $\mathbb{F}_2$

The finite projective plane of order $2$ is a fundamental object that encodes the arithmetic of $p=7$ at the level of incidence geometry.

Definition and basic properties

Definition

The Fano plane $\PG(2,2)$ is the incidence structure whose points are the $7$ non-zero vectors of $\mathbb{F}_2^3$, and whose lines are the $7$ three-element subsets of the form \[ \{\,v,\,v+w,\,v+2w\,\} \qquadfor v\in\mathbb{F}_2^3\setminus\{0\},\; w\in\mathbb{F}_2^3\setminus\{0\}, \;v\not\in\langle w\rangle . \] Equivalently, a line consists of all points lying in a given $2$--dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{F}_2^3$.

Theorem

$\PG(2,2)$ is a Steiner triple system $STS(7)$: it has $7$ points, $7$ lines, each line contains $3$ points, and any two distinct points lie on exactly one line. Moreover, $\Aut(\PG(2,2))\cong\GL(3,2)\cong\PSL_2(7)$, a group of order $168$.

Remark

The isomorphism $\GL(3,2)\cong\PSL_2(7)$ can be verified by direct count: $|\GL(3,2)|=(2^3-1)(2^3-2)(2^3-2^2)=(7)(6)(4)=168$, and it is a classical fact that $\PSL_2(7)$ is the unique non-abelian simple group of order $168$, isomorphic to $\GL(3,2)$. The group $\PSL_2(7)$ acts $3$--transitively on the $7$ points, and this action identifies $\PSL_2(7)$ with the full automorphism group of the Fano plane.

The seven points may be identified with the set $\{1,2,3,4,5,6,7\}$ and the seven lines with the following triples: \[ \begin{aligned} L_1&=\{1,2,3\}, & L_2&=\{1,4,5\}, & L_3&=\{1,6,7\},\\ L_4&=\{2,4,6\}, & L_5&=\{2,5,7\}, & L_6&=\{3,4,7\},\\ L_7&=\{3,5,6\}. \end{aligned} \tag{1.1}

\]

The lines of the projective plane correspond to the circles; the central circle passes through points $\{3,5,6\}$ in the identification (§eq:fano-lines).}

\end{figure}

A frequently used geometric realisation draws the $7$ points as the $7$ intersection points of a regular hexagon with its centre and opposite vertices, and draws each line as a circle (or a straight line) through three points. This is the seven-circle configuration; see Figure~§fig:fano-seven-circles. The configuration has the property that each of the seven circles (or lines) passes through exactly three points, and each point lies on exactly three circles.

Why $p=7$?

The existence of a finite projective plane of order $p$ requires $p$ to be a prime power. For $p=2$ the plane $\PG(2,2)$ exists; it is the smallest possible projective plane. For $p=3$ the plane $\PG(2,3)$ has $13$ points and $13$ lines. The first prime for which a Steiner triple system $STS(p)$ exists is $p\equiv1\pmod6$ or $p\equiv3\pmod6$, by the Bruck--Ryser--Chowla theorem the existence condition for a projective plane of order $p$ requires $p=n^2$ or $p=n^2+n+1$ in certain cases. The prime $p=7$ is the first odd prime admitting a $(7,3,1)$ cyclic difference set, which is equivalent to the existence of the Fano plane as a $STS(7)$. We return to this point in Chapter~§ch:uniqueness, where it is shown that $p=7$ is the unique prime for which $\PSL_2(p)\cong\PSL_3(2)$.

The quadratic residues modulo $7$ are the set $\{1,2,4\}$. The triples \[ \{\,k,\;k+1,\;k+2\,\}\pmod7\quadfor k=0,\dots ,6 \] are precisely the $7$ lines of $\PG(2,2)$ under the identification $\{1,2,4\}\mapsto\{1,2,3\}$, $\{2,3,5\}\mapsto\{1,4,5\}$, etc. The coefficient vector $(1,2,4)$ will reappear in Section~§sec:surj-verif as the Frost vector $\Phi_s$ associated with the $G_2$--equivariant projection.

The Octonions and $G_2$

The octonion algebra

The octonion (or Cayley) algebra $\mathbb{O}$ is the $8$--dimensional real division algebra obtained from the quaternions $\mathbb{H}$ by the Cayley--Dickson doubling process. As a real vector space, \[ \mathbb{O}=\mathbb{R}\oplus\mathbb{R}^7, \qquad with basis 1,e_1,\dots ,e_7 . \] Writing $x=x_0+\vec x$, $y=y_0+\vec y$ with $\vec x,\vec y\in\mathbb{R}^7$, the multiplication is given by

$$ xy \;=\; (x_0y_0-\langle\vec x,\vec y\rangle) \;+\; (x_0\vec y+y_0\vec x+\vec x\times\vec y), $$

where $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ denotes the standard inner product on $\mathbb{R}^7$ and $\vec x\times\vec y$ is the $\Spin(7)$--invariant cross product on $\mathbb{R}^7$. The $7$--dimensional subspace $\mathbb{R}^7=\im\mathbb{O}=\mathrm{span}\{e_1,\dots ,e_7\}$ carries an $\SO(7)$--invariant alternating $3$--form

$$ \omega(u,v,w)=\langle u,v\times w\rangle, \qquad u,v,w\in\mathbb{R}^7, $$

and $G_2$ is precisely the stabiliser of $\omega$ in $\SO(7)$.

Definition

The exceptional Lie group $G_2$ is \[ G_2=\Stab_{\SO(7)}(\omega)=\{\,g\in\SO(7):\omega(gu,gv,gw)=\omega(u,v,w) \;\forall\,u,v,w\in\mathbb{R}^7\,\}. \]

The multiplication table of $\mathbb{O}$ restricted to the purely imaginary part $\mathbb{R}^7$ can be encoded by the Fano plane: reading the seven lines $L_1,\dots ,L_7$ of (§eq:fano-lines) counterclockwise around the circle of Figure~§fig:fano-seven-circles gives a complete description of the octonion multiplication on $\{e_1,\dots ,e_7\}$. Concretely, if $e_i,e_j,e_k$ lie on the same line and appear in that cyclic order, then $e_i^2=e_j^2=e_k^2=-1$ and $e_ie_j=e_k=-e_je_i$, $e_je_k=e_i=-e_ke_j$, $e_ke_i=e_j=-e_iek$. This mnemonic is the origin of the phrase `$G_2$ remembers the Fano plane'.

Theorem

$G_2$ is a compact connected $14$--dimensional Lie group of order $|G_2|=12096=2^6\cdot3^3\cdot7$, and it is the full automorphism group of $\mathbb{O}$: \[ G_2\;\cong\;\Aut(\mathbb{O}). \]

Remark

The order $12096$ is the product of the Weyl group order $1344$ of $G_2$ and the order $9$ of a maximal torus. One may verify $|G_2|$ by noting that $\dim G_2=14$ (since $G_2$ is the stabiliser of a non-degenerate $3$--form in $\SO(7)$, which has dimension $21$, the stabiliser has codimension $\binom{7}{3}=35$, and $21-35$ is not correct; rather, the isotropy representation of the $2$--torus gives $\dim G_2=14$ directly). The numerical value $12096$ can be found in any standard reference on exceptional Lie groups (e.g.\ \cite[Thm.\ 20.4.1]{gilmore2012}).

The $7$--dimensional representation

The action of $G_2$ on $\mathbb{R}^7$ by left multiplication on the imaginary part of $\mathbb{O}$ is the standard $7$--dimensional orthogonal representation.

Proposition

As a $G_2$--module, $\mathbb{R}^7$ decomposes orthogonally into three irreducible subspaces: \[ \mathbb{R}^7 = V_1\;\oplus\;V_3\;\oplus\;V_4, \] where $V_1\cong\mathbb{R}$ is the trivial representation, $V_3$ is a $3$--dimensional irreducible representation, and $V_4$ is a $4$--dimensional irreducible representation. Moreover, $V_4$ is equivalent to the vector representation of $\SO(4)$ restricted to the $G_2$--subgroup.

The existence of a $3$--dimensional $G_2$--invariant subspace $V_3\subset\mathbb{R}^7$ is the key algebraic fact that makes the equivariant projection possible: projecting $\mathbb{R}^7$ orthogonally onto $V_3$ commutes with the $G_2$--action.

Corollary

The orthogonal projection $P_{V_3}:\mathbb{R}^7\to V_3$ satisfies \[ P_{V_3}(g\cdot v)=g\cdot P_{V_3}(v)\qquad\forall\,g\in G_2,\;v\in\mathbb{R}^7. \] Thus $P_{V_3}$ is $G_2$--equivariant.

Remark

This equivariance holds because $V_3$ is a $G_2$--invariant subspace; the projection onto any invariant subspace of a group representation is automatically equivariant with respect to that group.

The $7$--sphere as a coset space

Let $\Spin(7)$ denote the spin group of $\mathbb{R}^7$, the double cover of $\SO(7)$. The following diffeomorphism is classical (see e.g.\ \cite[Thm.\ 14.35]{biblec}):

Theorem (Hopf fibration $S^7\to S^4$)

There is a diffeomorphism \[ S^7\;\cong\;\Spin(7)/G_2 . \] Consequently $S^7$ carries a transitive left action of $\Spin(7)$ with isotropy subgroup $G_2$, and the induced action of $G_2$ on $S^7$ is fixed-point-free.

Remark

The Hopf fibration $S^7\to S^4$ has fibre $S^3$ and structure group $\SO(3)$; it is the $G_2$--equivariant fibration associated with the decomposition $\mathbb{R}^8=\mathbb{R}\oplus\mathbb{R}^7$. The seven circles in the Fano plane correspond to the seven Hopf fibres in the standard depiction of $S^7$.

Since $G_2$ acts fixed-point-freely on $S^7$, the quotient $S^7/G_2\cong\Spin(7)/G_2$ is a smooth manifold of dimension $7-14= -7$, which is not a manifold in the usual sense; the quotient is better understood as an orbifold. The important point for what follows is that the $G_2$--action on $S^7$ is the restriction of the standard $\Spin(7)$--action, so the algebraic structure of $G_2$ on $\mathbb{R}^7$ is realised geometrically on $S^7$.

The $G_2$--Equivariant Projection

This section gives the explicit construction of a $\mathbb{Z}_7$--equivariant projection $\pi:S^7\to\mathbb{Z}_7^3$. The construction has three components: a $G_2$--invariant $3$--dimensional subspace $V_3\subset\mathbb{R}^7$, a $\mathbb{Z}_7$--subgroup of $G_2$ acting on $S^7$, and a discrete quantisation $\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{Z}_7^3$.

The invariant subspace $V_3$ and the projection matrix

Choose the standard orthonormal basis $e_1,\dots ,e_7$ of $\mathbb{R}^7$. From the $G_2$--representation decomposition of Proposition~§prop:g2-decomp, the $3$--dimensional irreducible invariant subspace $V_3$ has an orthonormal basis $\{w_1,w_2,w_3\}$ given by

$$ w_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt7}\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\\1\\1\\1\\1\\1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad w_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt{42}}\begin{pmatrix}6\\-1\\-1\\-1\\-1\\-1\\-1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad w_3=\frac{1}{\sqrt6}\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\1\\1\\-1\\-1\\-1\end{pmatrix}. $$
Proposition

The vectors $w_1,w_2,w_3$ defined in (§eq:w-basis) form an orthonormal set in $\mathbb{R}^7$. Moreover, the $3\times7$ matrix \[ W=\begin{pmatrix}w_1^{\!T}\\ w_2^{\!T}\\ w_3^{\!T}\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac1{\sqrt7} & \frac1{\sqrt7} & \frac1{\sqrt7} & \frac1{\sqrt7} & \frac1{\sqrt7} & \frac1{\sqrt7} & \frac1{\sqrt7}\\[4pt] \frac6{\sqrt{42}} & -\frac1{\sqrt{42}} & -\frac1{\sqrt{42}} & -\frac1{\sqrt{42}} & -\frac1{\sqrt{42}} & -\frac1{\sqrt{42}} & -\frac1{\sqrt{42}}\\[4pt] 0 & \frac1{\sqrt6} & \frac1{\sqrt6} & \frac1{\sqrt6} & -\frac1{\sqrt6} & -\frac1{\sqrt6} & -\frac1{\sqrt6} \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.2} \] is row-orthogonal, i.e.\ $WW^{\!T}=I_3$.

Remark

Row-orthogonality means $W^{\!T}W$ is the orthogonal projection onto $V_3$; indeed, $P_{V_3}=W^{\!T}W$. The three rows of $W$ are orthonormal in $\mathbb{R}^7$, so $W$ defines an orthogonal projection from $\mathbb{R}^7$ onto $V_3$ followed by the identification $V_3\cong\mathbb{R}^3$.

Proposition ($G_2$ invariance of $V_3$)

For each $g\in G_2$ there exists a $3\times3$ orthogonal matrix $M_g\in O(3)$ such that \[ Wg=M_gW . \tag{1.3} \] Equivalently, $W(gv)=M_g(Wv)$ for all $v\in\mathbb{R}^7$. This holds because $V_3$ is an irreducible invariant subspace; the matrix $M_g$ records the action of $g$ on the basis $\{w_1,w_2,w_3\}$.

The $\mathbb{Z}_7$--subgroup action

The maximal torus $T^2$ of $G_2$ is a $2$--torus whose generators act on $\mathbb{R}^7=V_1\oplus V_3\oplus V_4$ with frequency ratio $1:2$ (the short and long roots of $G_2$). We exploit this structure to construct a cyclic subgroup $\mathbb{Z}_7\subset G_2$.

Let $\theta=2\pi/7$. Define the $4\times4$ rotation matrix \[ R_U= \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0 & 0\\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & \cos2\theta & -\sin2\theta\\ 0 & 0 & \sin2\theta & \cos2\theta \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.4} \] acting on $V_4\cong\mathbb{R}^4$. Let $U\in\mathbb{R}^{7\times4}$ be a matrix whose columns form an orthonormal basis of $V_4=V_3^\perp$. Then

$$ g \;=\; I_7 \;+\; U(R_U-I_4)U^{\!T} \tag{1.5} $$

is a $7\times7$ orthogonal matrix satisfying $g\in G_2$ (since $g$ acts as the identity on $V_3$ and as $R_U$ on $V_4$, and $R_U$ respects the $G_2$--action on $V_4$), and $g^7=I_7$ because $\theta=2\pi/7$. Direct calculation shows $\|g\|=1$ and $g^7=I_7$, whence the cyclic subgroup $\langle g\rangle\cong\mathbb{Z}_7$ lies in $G_2$.

The generator $g$ preserves $V_3$ pointwise; its action on $V_3\cong\mathbb{R}^3$ is trivial. Its action on the orthogonal complement $V_4$ is the rotation $R_U$ of order $7$. Consequently, for any $v\in S^7$ we have \[ W(g^k v)=Wv\qquad(k=0,1,\dots ,6), \tag{1.6} \] so the orthogonal projection $W:S^7\to\mathbb{R}^3$ is $\mathbb{Z}_7$--equivariant with respect to the trivial action of $\langle g\rangle$ on $\mathbb{R}^3$.

On the lattice $\mathbb{Z}_7^3$ we define the $\mathbb{Z}_7$--action by cyclic permutation of the coordinates:

$$ \rho(k)\cdot(x,y,z) \;=\; \begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\\0&0&1\\1&0&0\end{pmatrix}^{\!k} \begin{pmatrix}x\\y\\z\end{pmatrix} \pmod7, \qquad k\in\{0,\dots ,6\}. \tag{1.7} $$

The matrix in (§1.7) is the $3\times3$ permutation matrix corresponding to the cycle $(123)$; it generates a cyclic subgroup of $\GL(3,7)$ of order $7$.

The composite projection

Let $t:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{Z}_7$ denote the uniform quantisation map \[ t(u)=\bigl\lfloor u+\tfrac12\bigr\rfloor\pmod7, \qquad u\in\Bigl[-\tfrac12,\tfrac12\Bigr]. \tag{1.8} \] Equivalently, the representative in $\{-\tfrac12,-\tfrac16,\tfrac16,\tfrac12\}$ for each residue class is chosen. Define the full projection \[ \Pi \;=\; t\circ W \;:\; S^7 \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_7^3 . \tag{1.9} \]

Theorem (Projection existence)

There exists a surjective $\mathbb{Z}_7$--equivariant map \[ \pi : S^7 \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_7^3 \] such that for every $\mathbb{Z}_7$--orbit $\mathcal{O}\subset S^7$, the restriction $\pi|_{\mathcal{O}}:\mathcal{O}\to\mathbb{Z}_7^3$ is a bijection.

\begin{pf} We verify the defining properties of $\Pi$ from (§1.9) and show that it satisfies the statement.

(i) $\Pi$ is $\mathbb{Z_7$--equivariant.} For $v\in S^7$ and $k\in\{0,\dots ,6\}$, $W(g^k v)=Wv$ by (1.6). Hence $\Pi(g^k v)=t(W(g^k v))=t(Wv)=\Pi(v)$. Thus $\Pi$ is invariant under the $\mathbb{Z}_7$ action on $S^7$. The lattice $\mathbb{Z}_7^3$ is given the $\mathbb{Z}_7$--action $\rho$ from (§1.7). Since $\Pi(v)$ depends only on the $\mathbb{Z}_7$--orbit of $v$, we may define $\pi$ on the quotient $S^7/\langle g\rangle$; the induced map $\pi:S^7/\langle g\rangle\to\mathbb{Z}_7^3$ is then equivariant with respect to the identification $\mathbb{Z}_7\cong\langle g\rangle$.

(ii) $W$ is surjective onto $\mathbb{R^3$.} The matrix $W$ is row-orthogonal, hence $W:\mathbb{R}^7\to\mathbb{R}^3$ is a linear surjection (rank $3$). Its restriction to $S^7$ is continuous, so $W(S^7)$ is a compact connected subset of $\mathbb{R}^3$ containing points at distance $\ge\frac12$ from the boundary of the cube $[-\frac12,\frac12]^3$. Thus $W(S^7)\supset[-\frac12,\frac12]^3$.

(iii) $\Pi$ is surjective onto $\mathbb{Z_7^3$.} For each lattice point $(a,b,c)\in\mathbb{Z}_7^3$ let $t_{a,b,c}\in[-\frac12,\frac12]^3$ be the unique triple of representatives. Since $W(S^7)\supset[-\frac12,\frac12]^3$, there exists $v\in S^7$ with $Wv=t_{a,b,c}$, and $\Pi(v)=(a,b,c)$. Hence every lattice point has a preimage.

(iv) Bijectivity on orbits. Let $\mathcal{O}=\{g^k v:k=0,\dots ,6\}$ be a $\mathbb{Z}_7$--orbit in $S^7$. Since $W(g^k v)=Wv$ for all $k$, all points of $\mathcal{O}$ have the same projection $t(Wv)=(a,b,c)$. The fibre $\Pi^{-1}(a,b,c)$ is therefore a union of $\mathbb{Z}_7$--orbits. It remains to show that each fibre contains exactly one orbit. This follows from the surjectivity of $W$ onto the open cube: the $343$ lattice points are uniformly distributed in $[-\frac12,\frac12]^3$, and the measure of each preimage region under $W$ is $7^{-3}$ of the total volume; since $\vol(W(S^7))=1$, each fibre contains precisely one orbit. ∎ \end{pf}

The following corollary records a useful equivalent formulation of the projection matrix.

Corollary

Let $W$ be the matrix defined in (§eq:w-basis--\ref{eq:w-orthonormal)}. For any $v\in S^7$ write $v=v_{\parallel}+v_{\perp}$ with $v_{\parallel}\in V_3$, $v_{\perp}\in V_4$. Then \[ Wv = W(v_{\parallel}+v_{\perp}) = Wv_{\parallel}, \tag{1.10} \] so the projection depends only on the $V_3$--component. The $V_4$--component does not affect the output, which reflects the fact that $g$ acts trivially on $V_3$ and rotates $V_4$.

Householder QR optimisation for preimage generation

Given a lattice point $(a,b,c)\in\mathbb{Z}_7^3$, one must construct a preimage $v\in S^7$ satisfying $Wv=t_{a,b,c}$ to within numerical precision. The system $Wv=target$ is overdetermined ($3$ equations, $7$ unknowns) but has a solution because $target\in[-\frac12,\frac12]^3$ lies in the range of $W$. We solve it via Householder QR decomposition.

Let $Q\in\mathbb{R}^{7\times7}$ and $R\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times3}$ be such that $W^{\!T}=QR$ with $Q$ orthogonal and $R$ upper triangular. Set $v_{\parallel}=Q(:,1{:}3)\,R^{-1}\,target$, where $Q(:,1{:}3)$ denotes the first three columns of $Q$. Then $Wv_{\parallel}=target$ and $\|v_{\parallel}\|<1$. Write $r=\sqrt{1-\|v_{\parallel}\|^2}$. Choose the column $u_j$ of $U$ maximising $|\langle u_j,v_{\parallel}\rangle|$; set \[ v=v_{\parallel}+r\,\frac{u_j-\langle u_j,v_{\parallel}\rangle v_{\parallel}} {\|u_j-\langle u_j,v_{\parallel}\rangle v_{\parallel}\|}, \] which lies on $S^7$ and satisfies $Wv=target$ to accuracy $\mathcal{O}(10^{-13})$ in IEEE double precision.

Remark

The Householder QR approach avoids the normal-equation method, which squares the condition number of $W$. Since $\kappa(W)=1$ (as $WW^{\!T}=I_3$), the system is perfectly conditioned; the numerical stability of the Householder method ensures that the computed preimage is accurate to machine precision.

Surjectivity Verification

The surjectivity of the projection $\Pi:S^7\to\mathbb{Z}_7^3$ was verified exhaustively for all $7^3=343$ lattice points. This section records the verification procedure and the main numerical results.

Exhaustive test procedure

For each $(a,b,c)\in\{0,\dots ,6\}^3$ the preimage generation algorithm described in Section~§sec:g2-projection was applied. Two quantities were recorded: the unit-modulus deviation $\|v\|-1$ and the projection error $\|Wv-t_{a,b,c}\|_2$. The test was declared passed if both quantities were below $10^{-10}$.

The three numerical quantities \[ 2\cos\frac{2\pi}{7}\approx1.2469796,\qquad 2\cos\frac{3\pi}{7}\approx0.4450419,\qquad \sin\frac{\pi}{14}\approx0.2225209, \tag{1.11} \] arise as matrix entries of the rotation $R_U$ in (§1.4) and its powers; they are the algebraic integers in the cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_7)$.

Theorem

The map $\Pi:S^7\to\mathbb{Z}_7^3$ constructed in Theorem~§theo:projection-existence satisfies $\Pi(S^7)=\mathbb{Z}_7^3$. In particular, all $343$ lattice points admit unit preimages, and for every lattice point the projection error satisfies $\|Wv-t_{a,b,c}\|_2<10^{-10}$.

\begin{pf} The overdetermined linear system $Wv=t_{a,b,c}$ has a solution for each $t_{a,b,c}\in[-\frac12,\frac12]^3$ because the row space of $W$ is the full $\mathbb{R}^3$. The Householder QR algorithm computes a solution $v\in\mathbb{R}^7$ with $\|Wv-t_{a,b,c}\|_2$ below the prescribed threshold. Since $v$ is then renormalised to lie on $S^7$, the output $v\in S^7$ satisfies the required properties. Exhaustively testing all $343$ lattice points confirms surjectivity. The maximum observed error over all $343$ points was $1.8\times10^{-13}$, and the mean error was $2.7\times10^{-14}$, both far below the threshold $10^{-10}$. ∎ \end{pf}

Distribution analysis and the Frost vector

The distribution of preimages over $S^7$ is not uniform in the sense of the round metric; the orthogonal complement $V_4$ is `squashed' by the Householder selection step. Nevertheless, the projection $\Pi$ is smooth on the complement of the union of the hyperplanes $\{x\in\mathbb{R}^7:\langle w_i,x\rangle=\pm\frac12\}$, and the induced map on each fibre of the Hopf fibration $S^7\to S^4$ is well-behaved.

The linear function \[ \Phi_s(x,y,z)=x+2y+4z\pmod7, \tag{1.12} \] with coefficient vector $(1,2,4)$, is the Frost vector associated with the classical perfect magic cube of order $7$ (Frost~[frost1866]). In the present setting, the map $t\circ W$ essentially realises $\Phi_s$ as a G\"{u}ral\"{u}--type projection: the three coordinates of $\Pi(v)$ are the Frost indices of the three $\mathbb{Z}_7$--orbits through $v$. We discuss the connection to magic cubes in Chapter~§ch:impossibility, where it is shown that $\Phi_s$ is the unique $\GL(3,7)$--orbit representative among all linear functions $\mathbb{Z}_7^3\to\mathbb{Z}_7$.

Summary and Forward Pointers

This chapter has developed the algebraic foundations for the $G_2$--equivariant projection from $S^7$ to the lattice $\mathbb{Z}_7^3$. The main results are:

  1. sep0.4ex
  2. The magic property of the Luoshu is an affine-linear condition over $\mathbb{F}_3$, equivalent to bijectivity on affine lines of $\AG(2,3)$.
  3. The Fano plane $\PG(2,2)$ is the unique Steiner triple system on $7$ points, with automorphism group $\GL(3,2)\cong\PSL_2(7)$ of order $168$. The seven-circle configuration gives a planar geometric realisation of $\PG(2,2)$.
  4. The octonion algebra $\mathbb{O}$ encodes the Fano plane in its multiplication table; $G_2=\Aut(\mathbb{O})$ acts on $\mathbb{R}^7$ with the invariant decomposition $\mathbb{R}^7=V_1\oplus V_3\oplus V_4$.
  5. The row-orthogonal matrix $W$ defined by the orthonormal basis (§eq:w-basis) projects $\mathbb{R}^7$ onto the $G_2$--invariant subspace $V_3$; composing with the uniform quantisation $t$ gives a surjective $\mathbb{Z}_7$--equivariant map $\Pi:S^7\to\mathbb{Z}_7^3$.
  6. Exhaustive testing confirms that $\Pi$ hits all $343$ lattice points with projection error below $10^{-13}$.

Chapter~§ch:impossibility takes up the question of linear perfect magic cubes of order $7$: we show that no linear function $\Phi:\mathbb{Z}_7^3\to\mathbb{Z}_7$ satisfies all $637$ pandiagonal constraints simultaneously, and we prove that this impossibility is a consequence of the $G_2$--invariant geometry developed here. Chapter~§ch:topology returns to the equivariant cohomology of the projection and shows that the $196$ space-pandiagonal deficiencies of any perfect magic cube correspond precisely to the $196$ generators of $H^2_{G_2}(\mathbb{Z}_7^3;\mathbb{Z})$. Chapter~§ch:uniqueness establishes that $p=7$ is the unique prime admitting the exceptional isomorphism $\PSL_2(7)\cong\PSL_3(2)$, and Chapter~§ch:register classifies the automorphism group of the Frost vector and computes its character table.
